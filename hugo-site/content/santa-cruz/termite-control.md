Termite identification matters because not every colony behaves the same way. In this part of California, the two most common concerns are subterranean termites and drywood termites. Subterranean termites live in the soil and travel into structures through shelter tubes. They need moisture and usually enter through foundations, cracks, plumbing penetrations, or wood that touches the ground. These termites can cause extensive structural damage because the colony may be large and active for a long time before visible signs appear.

Drywood termites are different. They do not need soil contact. They infest dry, sound wood and can live inside wall studs, attic framing, fascia boards, trim, eaves, and furniture. Instead of mud tubes, they leave behind small six-sided fecal pellets, often called frass, near kick-out holes. Because they can live entirely inside the wood they are eating, drywood termites are often missed until damage shows up during remodeling, painting, or a real estate inspection.

Proper identification starts with the basics. Winged swarmers are often the first thing people notice. Termite swarmers have straight antennae, a broad waist, and two pairs of wings that are the same length. Ant swarmers, which are commonly mistaken for termites, have elbowed antennae, a narrow waist, and front wings that are longer than the back wings. Misidentification is one reason store-bought sprays fail. Treating the wrong insect wastes time while the real issue continues inside the structure.

Common signs of a termite infestation include:

Discarded wings on window sills, near doors, or around light sources after a swarm.
Mud tubes along foundations, stem walls, crawl space piers, or garage slabs.
Wood that sounds hollow when tapped.
Blistered, rippled, or soft-looking paint and drywall surfaces.
Tight-fitting doors or windows caused by subtle frame distortion.
Frass piles that look like coarse sand or pepper under drywood kick-out holes.
Baseboards, trim, or exposed wood that crumbles when probed.

Homes near the coast in Santa Cruz often deal with added moisture exposure from marine air, damp crawl spaces, and shaded exterior areas that stay wet longer. That does not create termites by itself, but it does make conditions more favorable, especially for subterranean activity and for wood decay that can hide termite access points. If you have had plumbing leaks, poor drainage, standing water near the foundation, or old untreated wood outdoors, your risk is higher.

Many people ask whether termites are a health risk. Termites are not known for spreading disease the way cockroaches, rodents, or mosquitoes can. The real danger is structural. Termites weaken framing, subfloors, support posts, window headers, roof members, and wood trim. Left untreated, that damage can create unsafe floors, sticking doors, sagging sections, and expensive repair work. In some cases, damaged wood also traps moisture, which can contribute to secondary issues such as fungal growth. Drywood frass and disturbed dust from damaged wall voids may also aggravate indoor air concerns for sensitive occupants, but the primary threat is to the building itself.

That is why timing matters. In California, termite pressure is not limited to one short season. Activity can continue year-round, especially in mild coastal conditions. Still, there are times when infestations are more commonly discovered. Late winter through spring is a major inspection window for subterranean termites, particularly after rainfall followed by warmer days. April is an especially important month because swarmers begin showing up, and property owners notice fresh signs as termites become more active. For drywood termites, swarming often becomes more noticeable during warmer periods from summer into early fall, though infestations can be found at any time of year. The practical takeaway is simple: spring is the best time to catch many problems early, but treatment should be scheduled as soon as evidence appears, regardless of the month.

Treatment options depend on the termite species, the location of activity, and whether the infestation is localized or widespread.

For drywood termites in limited areas, spot treatment may be appropriate. This can include drilling into affected voids and applying foam or termiticide materials directly into galleries. In some cases, borate-based wood treatments are used on exposed wood to kill active termites and add residual protection. Spot treatment works best when infestation areas are clearly identified and isolated.

For larger or widespread drywood infestations, whole-structure fumigation may be recommended. This is typically the most effective choice when termites are present in multiple rooms, inaccessible voids, attic framing, or throughout older wood members. Fumigation addresses the entire structure at once, reaching areas that localized products may miss. It does not provide ongoing residual protection after the process is complete, so prevention steps still matter afterward.

For subterranean termites, soil-applied liquid treatments and baiting systems are the standard solutions. A liquid treatment may involve trenching and, where needed, drilling around foundation areas to create a treated zone that termites cannot cross without exposure. Bait systems are installed around the structure and monitored over time, allowing termites to carry active ingredients back to the colony. In some cases, we recommend a combination approach, especially when site conditions, drainage, or construction details make one method stronger than the other.

For exposed wood at risk but not yet heavily damaged, preventive wood treatments can help reduce future vulnerability. This is often useful during repairs, remodels, or when damaged trim, fascia, or subfloor sections are being replaced.

After active termites are eliminated, damaged wood should be evaluated for repair or replacement. A successful termite job is not just about killing insects. It is about making sure the structure is sound and less likely to be reinfested.
