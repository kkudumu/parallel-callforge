## What Are Silverfish?

Silverfish are small, wingless insects. They grow about half an inch to one inch long. They have a silver or gray color and a teardrop shape. They move fast — darting side to side like a fish. That's how they got their name.

They prefer:

- Bathrooms and laundry rooms
- Kitchens and pantries
- Basements and crawl spaces
- Attics and wall voids
- Closets and storage areas
- Anywhere paper, cardboard, or fabric is stored

Silverfish are nocturnal. You rarely see them during the day. If you do spot one in daylight, that's a red flag. The infestation may already be large.

## Signs You Have a Silverfish Problem

You don't always see the bugs themselves first. Look for these warning signs in your home:

**Yellow stains or scales** — Small yellow stains on fabric, paper, or walls. These come from silverfish droppings or shed skins.

**Tiny holes in paper or fabric** — Silverfish chew irregular patterns into books, wallpaper, clothing, and cardboard boxes. The edges look ragged and uneven.

**Shed skins** — Silverfish shed their skin repeatedly throughout their life. Finding small, hollow shells is a clear sign of activity.

**Damage to stored belongings** — Unexplained damage to books, photos, old clothing, or documents in storage is a common clue.

**Musty odor in enclosed spaces** — A damp, musty smell in closets, attics, or basements can signal a silverfish hot spot.

## Are Silverfish Dangerous?

Silverfish don't bite. They don't sting. They don't carry disease the way rodents do. But don't mistake that for harmless.

Here's the real damage they cause:

**Allergic reactions** — Silverfish shed scales and leave droppings behind. These particles can trigger skin rashes and respiratory irritation in sensitive people, including children.

**Food contamination** — Silverfish get into dry pantry goods — flour, oats, cereals, pasta. Anything they touch is contaminated.

**Property destruction** — Books, photos, wallpaper, clothing, and important documents are all targets. A large infestation can cause significant financial loss over time.

**A moisture warning sign** — Silverfish need humidity to survive. If they've moved in, you have a moisture problem. Moisture attracts other pests too — including cockroaches and centipedes.

## Why Silverfish Are a Year-Round Problem in Florida

Florida's climate is perfect for silverfish. Humidity is high. Warmth is constant. That's everything a silverfish needs to breed and thrive.

In Deland, homeowners face silverfish pressure every month of the year. Summer brings peak humidity and fast breeding cycles. But Florida's mild winters give silverfish no reason to slow down. They stay active even when temperatures drop.

## How to Prevent Silverfish From Coming Back

Treatment stops the current infestation. Prevention stops the next one. Here's what works:

**Fix leaks right away** — A dripping pipe under the sink is an open invitation. Silverfish will find it.

**Run a dehumidifier** — Keep indoor humidity below 50 percent. This removes one of their most basic survival needs.

**Seal cracks and gaps** — Use caulk around baseboards, pipe penetrations, and windows. Small openings are how they get in.

**Switch to plastic storage bins** — Cardboard boxes hold moisture and give silverfish something to eat. Sealed plastic bins protect stored belongings and remove the food source.

**Clear out clutter** — Stacks of old newspapers, magazines, and boxes are ideal silverfish habitat. If you haven't touched it in a year, clean it out.

**Ventilate attics and crawl spaces** — Good airflow keeps these areas dry. That alone reduces silverfish pressure significantly.

**Inspect storage areas regularly** — Don't let infestations develop unseen. Check closets, attics, and under-sink cabinets a few times a year.
