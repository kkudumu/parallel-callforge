By the time you spot one crawling across a bathroom floor at midnight or find yellowed, chewed edges on old books stored in the closet, there is a good chance dozens more are hiding in walls, under flooring, and inside storage areas. ## What Are Silverfish?

Silverfish (*Lepisma saccharina*) are small, wingless insects named for their metallic, silvery appearance and the wriggling, fish-like way they move. Adults grow between half an inch and one inch long, with a tapered body, three long tail filaments at the rear, and two long antennae up front. They are fast movers and strongly prefer darkness, which is why most homeowners rarely see them despite having large populations living in the home.

They feed primarily on carbohydrates and proteins, which means almost anything starchy or cellulose-based is on the menu:

- Books, magazines, newspapers, and cardboard
- Wallpaper and wallpaper adhesive
- Natural-fiber clothing such as cotton, linen, silk, and wool
- Stored pantry items including flour, oats, and dry cereals
- Photographs, important documents, and printed materials
- Glue bindings on books and boxes

Silverfish are nocturnal and hide during daylight hours in narrow cracks, behind baseboards, and inside wall voids. Most infestations are far larger than what is visible on the surface.

## Signs of a Silverfish Infestation

Spotting a single silverfish is worth taking seriously. Here is what to look for throughout your home:

**Visible insects at night.** Finding silverfish in bathtubs, sinks, or crawling along baseboards after dark is the clearest indicator. They are drawn to moisture, so bathrooms, laundry rooms, and kitchens are common first-sighting locations.

**Feeding damage on paper and fabric.** Silverfish scrape surfaces rather than chewing cleanly, so damage appears as irregular holes, scraped patches, or scalloped edges on books, wallpaper, clothing, and stored documents. Yellowing on paper that is not explained by age alone can also be a sign.

**Small pepper-like droppings.** Silverfish feces are tiny, round, and black — similar in size to ground black pepper. Look for them behind baseboards, inside closets, and beneath cardboard boxes on storage shelves.

**Shed skins.** Silverfish molt repeatedly throughout their lives, sometimes more than 50 times. Finding thin, translucent, silvery skin casings in corners, storage areas, or behind furniture is a reliable indicator that silverfish are active in that area.

**Yellow staining.** Some surfaces that silverfish contact regularly develop a faint yellowish residue. Unexplained yellow marks on fabric or paper in storage areas can point toward silverfish activity.

## Health Risks and Property Damage

Silverfish do not bite and are not known to spread disease to humans directly. However, their shed skins and droppings can trigger allergic reactions in people with respiratory sensitivities — and in Florida homes where dust mites or mold are already a concern, a silverfish infestation compounds those indoor air quality issues.

The more immediate concern is property damage. Silverfish destroy things that cannot easily be replaced: family photos, handwritten documents, heirloom clothing, and bookshelves built up over a lifetime. In homes with older construction or persistent humidity issues — both common across Volusia County — silverfish can become embedded in walls, attic insulation, and crawl space materials before homeowners realize the extent of the problem.

A female silverfish can lay two to 20 eggs at a time and live anywhere from two to eight years. In a home with adequate moisture and accessible food sources, populations build steadily month after month without visible signs until the infestation is well established.

## Treatment Options for Silverfish

Effective silverfish control requires more than a single spray application. An established infestation needs a layered approach, and a good treatment plan addresses both the population and the conditions sustaining it.

**Inspection and source identification.** Before any product is applied, a licensed technician should walk the home to map active areas, locate harborage zones, and identify moisture or food source issues. Skipping this step is why many DIY attempts and low-effort treatments fail to hold.

**Interior perimeter treatment.** Residual insecticide applications along baseboards, behind appliances, in closets, and in other areas where silverfish travel form the core of an effective program. Products applied to surfaces where silverfish walk are far more effective than broadcast sprays into open air.

**Crack and crevice application.** Silverfish hide in narrow gaps behind trim, inside wall voids, and beneath bathroom fixtures. Targeted crack and crevice applications reach hiding places that surface treatments miss entirely.

**Dust applications in wall voids and attics.** In moderate to heavy infestations, applying insecticidal dust into wall voids, attic spaces, or crawl spaces can significantly reduce populations that live inside the structure itself. This is particularly relevant in older homes with more open wall construction.

**Moisture reduction guidance.** No treatment holds long-term if the conditions drawing silverfish in are not corrected. A thorough technician will point out areas of excessive humidity and recommend specific corrective steps — whether that means improving ventilation, using a dehumidifier, or repairing a plumbing leak.

**Follow-up treatment.** Silverfish eggs are resistant to most insecticides and hatch over a period of weeks. A follow-up visit several weeks after initial treatment is frequently necessary to address newly hatched insects before they mature and reproduce.

## Seasonal Timing for Silverfish in the Deland Area

Florida's climate creates year-round silverfish pressure — the mild winters that slow pest activity in northern states do not significantly reduce silverfish populations here. That said, activity does shift with the seasons in ways that are worth understanding.

**Spring and Summer (March through September).** Humidity climbs sharply. This is when silverfish populations expand most aggressively. Warm, wet conditions inside crawl spaces, under bathroom fixtures, and near exterior walls give silverfish ideal breeding conditions. If you have never noticed them before and suddenly spot one during the rainy season, rising humidity is almost always the trigger.

**Fall (October and November).** As temperatures moderate slightly, silverfish that have been living near exterior walls and in crawl spaces may move deeper into interior living spaces. This is a common time for homeowners to first notice a problem that has been quietly building for months.

**Winter (December through February).** Silverfish do not go dormant. They continue feeding and reproducing through Florida winters. Homes that are heated heavily during cooler stretches may actually see increased activity, as warm indoor conditions offset the slightly lower outdoor humidity.

Year-round treatment and prevention matter here. Waiting until spring to address a silverfish problem means giving them months of additional time to establish and spread.

## Prevention: Reducing the Conditions That Invite Silverfish

Professional treatment handles the existing population, but prevention is what keeps them from coming back.

**Control indoor humidity.** Run exhaust fans in bathrooms and kitchens during and after use. Consider a dehumidifier for basement, crawl space, or laundry areas. Silverfish thrive when humidity exceeds 50 percent — keeping levels below that threshold removes one of their core requirements.

**Seal entry points.** Caulk gaps around plumbing penetrations, windows, door frames, and where utility lines enter the structure. Silverfish can move through very small openings, so attention to detail matters here.

**Store paper goods and pantry items properly.** Important documents, photographs, and books stored in damp areas should be moved to sealed plastic containers. Pantry staples like flour, oats, and cereals should be in airtight containers rather than open bags or loosely closed boxes.

**Reduce clutter in storage areas.** Silverfish prefer undisturbed piles of boxes, books, and clothing. Regularly reorganizing storage areas — and removing cardboard boxes that have sat in the same spot for years — eliminates the harborage zones they rely on.

**Fix plumbing leaks without delay.** A slow drip under a kitchen sink or a weeping pipe fitting in a crawl space creates exactly the moist environment silverfish need. Addressing leaks promptly removes a significant attractant.

**Inspect items brought into the home.** Secondhand books, furniture purchased at estate sales, and boxes pulled from outdoor storage can introduce silverfish to a home that was previously clear. A quick inspection before bringing items inside is a simple habit that prevents a lot of headaches.
