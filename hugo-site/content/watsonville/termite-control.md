Identification: Know Which Termite You Are Dealing With

Effective treatment begins with proper identification. Different termite species behave differently, nest in different places, and require different control methods.

Subterranean termites are the most destructive species found in many California service areas. They live in the soil and build mud tubes to travel between the ground and the wood they are feeding on. Because they need moisture, they are commonly found around foundations, crawl spaces, utility penetrations, and areas where leaks or drainage issues keep materials damp. Damage from subterranean termites often follows the grain of the wood and can weaken framing over time.

Drywood termites do not need direct contact with soil. They live inside the wood they infest, which means they can establish in attic framing, fascia boards, window trim, siding, exposed beams, and furniture. These termites are often discovered when homeowners notice small piles of pellet-like droppings, also called frass, below infested wood. Unlike subterranean termites, drywood colonies are usually contained within the wood itself, but they can still cause serious damage if the infestation spreads.

A professional inspection separates termites from other wood-destroying pests such as carpenter ants, powderpost beetles, or wood rot caused by moisture. That distinction matters, because the wrong treatment wastes time and money while the real problem continues.

Common Signs of a Termite Infestation

Most termite problems are found after the colony has been active for some time. The insects stay out of sight, so the warning signs are usually indirect. Property owners should watch for:

Swarming termites indoors or around windows and doors. Swarms often happen when a colony is mature and produces winged reproductives. Homeowners sometimes mistake them for flying ants, but termite swarmers have straight antennae, broad waists, and equal-length wings.

Discarded wings on window sills, in garages, near light fixtures, or around entry points. After swarming, termites shed their wings and look for a place to start a new colony.

Mud tubes along foundations, stem walls, piers, or crawl space supports. These pencil-width tubes are a strong indicator of subterranean termite activity.

Wood that sounds hollow when tapped. Termites eat from the inside out, so boards can look normal on the surface while the interior is badly damaged.

Blistered paint, bubbling drywall, tight-fitting doors, or warped trim. These symptoms are often blamed on moisture alone, but hidden termite activity can be part of the problem.

Small piles of dry pellets beneath wood members, window frames, or attic lumber. This is one of the clearest signs of drywood termites.

Baseboards, flooring, porch posts, or trim that crumbles easily when probed. In advanced infestations, the damage becomes visible once the surface breaks.

Are Termites a Health Risk?

Termites are not known for biting people the way bed bugs or mosquitoes do, and they are not typically linked to direct disease transmission. Still, they create real health and safety concerns in a home or business.

The biggest risk is structural. As termites hollow out framing, subfloors, joists, fascia, and support members, they can weaken the building itself. Over time, damaged wood can create unsafe floors, sagging areas, failing trim, and costly repair issues. In severe cases, termite damage contributes to unsafe living conditions.

Termite activity also often overlaps with excess moisture. Leaks, poor drainage, and damp crawl spaces attract subterranean termites and can also support mold growth. That means a termite problem may be exposing a larger moisture issue that affects indoor air quality.

Another concern is misapplied do-it-yourself products. Store-bought sprays may kill a few visible insects, but they rarely eliminate the colony. Improper use of pesticides inside living spaces, attics, or crawl spaces can create unnecessary exposure for people and pets without solving the infestation.

The bottom line is simple: termites may not be a direct medical pest, but they are a serious property risk that can affect safety, sanitation, and repair costs if they are ignored.

Professional Treatment Options

There is no one-size-fits-all termite service. The right treatment depends on the termite species, where the activity is located, how widespread the infestation is, and whether there is old damage, active feeding, or both.

For subterranean termites, liquid soil treatments are one of the most common solutions. A technician treats the soil around the foundation and other key entry points to create a treated zone that termites cannot cross without being eliminated. In some cases, trenching or drilling is needed around slabs, garage edges, porches, or masonry features to reach the correct areas.

Baiting systems are another option for subterranean termites. Bait stations are installed in the soil around the structure and monitored regularly. Worker termites feed on the bait and carry it back to the colony, which can reduce or eliminate colony activity over time. Baiting can be a strong long-term management tool, especially where ongoing monitoring is important.

For localized drywood termite infestations, spot treatments may be effective. These treatments target specific areas of active infestation inside accessible wood members. Depending on the situation, treatment may involve injection into wall voids or directly into infested wood.

When drywood termites are spread through multiple areas of a structure, whole-structure fumigation may be recommended. Fumigation is designed to eliminate drywood termites throughout the building, including hidden colonies in inaccessible framing. It is often the most complete option when activity is widespread or recurring.

In some cases, damaged wood must also be repaired or replaced after treatment. Termite control and carpentry repairs are separate issues, but both may be needed to fully restore the property.

Seasonal Timing for Termite Control in California

Termites are active year-round in California, but some seasons are better for spotting activity and preventing larger infestations.

Spring is one of the busiest seasons for termite discovery. As temperatures rise and moisture conditions shift, swarms become more noticeable. April is an especially important month for inspections because that is when many property owners first see swarmers, discarded wings, or fresh signs around windows, crawl spaces, and foundations. In California, April is the time to move quickly on prevention and treatment before colonies expand further into the structure.

Summer can still be active, especially for drywood termites in attic spaces, fascia, eaves, and sun-exposed wood. Warm weather also makes it easier to inspect exterior wood trim, fences, detached structures, and roofline components.

Fall is a good time to catch problems before winter moisture sets in. Inspections during this period often uncover drainage issues, wood-to-soil contact, and exterior cracks that can support subterranean termite activity.

Winter does not stop termites. In mild coastal conditions, they remain active even when homeowners assume pest pressure is lower. That makes year-round inspections important, especially if you have had prior activity, high moisture, or older wood construction.

For most homes, an annual termite inspection is a smart baseline. Properties with a history of infestation, crawl space moisture, or prior repairs may need more frequent monitoring.

Prevention: How to Lower the Risk of Future Infestations

The best termite control program combines treatment with prevention. After the active problem is addressed, the next step is making the property less attractive to termites.

Fix leaks quickly. Plumbing drips, roof leaks, and faulty irrigation create the moisture conditions that subterranean termites need.

Keep soil and mulch from covering siding, weep screeds, or wood trim. Wood-to-soil contact gives termites an easy entry point.

Maintain proper drainage. Downspouts should move water away from the foundation, and crawl spaces should stay as dry as possible.

Store firewood, lumber, cardboard, and cellulose debris away from the structure. These materials can attract termites and other pests.

Seal gaps around utility lines, foundation cracks, and exterior penetrations where feasible.

Schedule routine inspections, especially if the home is older, has a crawl space, or has had previous termite work.

Do not rely on over-the-counter sprays. They may kill exposed insects but will not solve hidden activity in soil, framing, or inaccessible wood.
