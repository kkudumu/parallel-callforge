Silverfish and clothes moths are quiet destroyers. They feed on fabric, stored food, and paper—and you often do not notice until the damage is done. In Shawnee homes, silverfish are a recurring pest problem found in basements, bathroom vanities, closets, and anywhere moisture and undisturbed storage create the conditions they need to thrive.

If you are finding holes in books, discoloration on stored fabrics, or damaged wallpaper edges, silverfish are a likely cause. Here is what local homeowners need to know—and what to do about it.

## What Silverfish Look Like

Silverfish are small, wingless insects, typically half to three-quarters of an inch long. The body is covered in metallic silver scales and tapers from a wider head to a pointed rear end, giving the insect its fish-like shape. Three thin bristle-like appendages extend from the tail end. When startled, they move in a fast, wriggling side-to-side motion that can surprise homeowners who find one crossing a bathroom floor late at night.

Unlike ants or cockroaches, silverfish do not form visible colonies in the open. They stay hidden during the day inside wall voids, behind baseboards, under stored boxes, and within stacks of paper and books. Most infestations build steadily for weeks or months before homeowners notice enough activity to raise concern.

### Silverfish vs. Firebrats

Firebrats look similar to silverfish but have a mottled gray-brown coloration rather than uniform silver scaling. They prefer very high heat—furnace rooms, areas adjacent to ovens, and other hot spots throughout the home. Silverfish favor cool-to-moderate temperatures and elevated humidity. Both species cause comparable types of material damage and respond to similar treatment approaches, but correctly identifying which species is present helps a technician focus on the right harborage zones.

## Signs of a Silverfish Infestation

Because silverfish are nocturnal and avoid light, direct sightings are often not the first indicator of a problem. Most homeowners notice the evidence left behind before spotting an actual insect.

Look for these warning signs inside the home:

- **Irregular holes or scrape marks** in books, wallpaper, cardboard boxes, or packaged food
- **Yellowish staining** on paper products, fabric, or stored documents
- **Shed skins** in undisturbed storage areas, closet corners, or behind boxes—silverfish molt repeatedly throughout their lifespan
- **Small, dark, pepper-like droppings** found near active feeding zones
- **Peeled or notched wallpaper edges** in bathrooms, hallways, or storage rooms with poor air circulation

Finding shed skins is one of the clearest signs that silverfish have established a harborage inside the home. The location of those skins tells a technician exactly where to concentrate inspection and treatment efforts.

## Are Silverfish a Health Risk?

Silverfish do not bite people and are not known to transmit disease the way cockroaches or rodents can. That said, they are not entirely harmless pests.

**Allergen exposure.** Silverfish produce a protein called tropomyosin. Their shed scales and fecal matter become airborne particulates in enclosed spaces and can aggravate respiratory symptoms in people with existing allergies or asthma. This is particularly relevant when silverfish are active near HVAC return vents or in finished basement spaces where air circulates throughout the home.

**Property and material damage.** The more immediate concern is the destruction of belongings. Silverfish consume starches, sugars, and proteins found in paper, fabric, adhesive bookbindings, pantry staples, and stored keepsakes. Left untreated through a full season, an active population can cause significant and often irreversible damage to items that cannot be replaced.

**Moisture indicators.** A home that supports silverfish is typically harboring enough moisture to also support mold growth or additional pest pressure. Silverfish activity is frequently a signal that a larger underlying condition—plumbing leak, ventilation gap, crawl space humidity problem—needs to be corrected alongside the pest treatment.

## Seasonal Activity for Silverfish in Kansas

Silverfish can remain active indoors year-round, but their behavior follows seasonal patterns that affect when inspections and treatments produce the best results.

### Spring (March–May)

As outdoor humidity climbs in the Kansas City region, silverfish that overwintered in wall voids and crawl spaces become more active and mobile. Homeowners begin spotting them in bathrooms and kitchens as the insects migrate upward from lower, damper parts of the structure in search of food sources.

### Summer (June–August)

Hot, humid summer conditions push silverfish toward climate-controlled interior spaces. Finished basements, air-conditioned storage rooms, and interior closets become prime harborage zones. Book collections, fabric storage, and paper archives face the highest risk for material damage during these months.

### Fall (September–November)

Cooling temperatures drive silverfish to seek protected locations where they can survive through winter. Fall is a high-activity period for finding silverfish in previously undisturbed areas—attic storage, wardrobe boxes, and basement shelving that has not been accessed since spring. September is one of the busiest months for pest migration indoors, as conditions outside become increasingly unfavorable.

### Winter (December–February)

Cold weather slows silverfish reproduction but does not eliminate an established infestation. Indoor environments maintain enough warmth near plumbing fixtures, water heaters, and HVAC components to keep populations alive and active. A silverfish problem discovered in January or February has typically been developing quietly for several months before becoming visible.

## Professional Treatment Options

### Inspection First

Effective silverfish control starts with a thorough inspection—not a quick surface spray. A licensed technician should identify where activity is concentrated, what materials the insects are feeding on, and what structural or moisture conditions are allowing the population to persist. Treating visible areas without addressing source conditions produces limited results and typically leads to recurring activity.

### Targeted Residual Applications

Crack-and-crevice applications of residual insecticide in high-activity zones—baseboards, under sinks, along pipe chases, inside cabinet voids, and in storage areas—are the primary treatment method for established silverfish populations. These products remain active for several weeks and eliminate silverfish as they travel through treated areas. All products used in residential settings are EPA-registered and selected to minimize exposure risk around children and pets.

### Insecticidal Dust Treatments

In wall voids, attic spaces, and crawl spaces, insecticidal dusts provide long-lasting control in locations where liquid applications are not practical or effective. Dusts adhere to surfaces and remain active far longer than sprays, making them well-suited for reaching harborage zones inside structural voids where silverfish rest and reproduce.

### Moisture Reduction

No treatment program produces lasting results without addressing the humidity conditions that support the infestation. A complete treatment plan typically includes specific recommendations for dehumidifier placement, ventilation corrections, and identification of any plumbing leaks or HVAC condensation issues contributing to elevated humidity. Reducing relative indoor humidity to below 50% is one of the most effective long-term controls available for silverfish.

### Follow-Up Service

Established infestations in older structures or homes with significant moisture issues typically require more than one treatment visit. A follow-up inspection three to four weeks after initial treatment confirms whether activity has been reduced, identifies any missed harborage zones, and allows for retreatment where needed.

## Preventing Silverfish from Coming Back

Once an infestation has been treated, these measures significantly reduce the conditions that invite silverfish back:

**Control moisture first.** Run a dehumidifier in basement and crawl space areas where relative humidity consistently exceeds 55%. Fix dripping pipes promptly and confirm that bathroom exhaust fans vent to the exterior—not into attic space—where moisture can accumulate.

**Switch from cardboard to plastic storage.** Cardboard absorbs moisture and serves as a direct food source and shelter material for silverfish. Move books, documents, and stored clothing into sealed plastic bins on shelving units elevated off concrete floors.

**Seal entry points.** Silverfish enter through gaps around pipe penetrations, between siding and foundation, and through cracks in exterior walls. Caulking and sealing these gaps limits migration from exterior harborage areas into the home's interior.

**Reduce clutter in storage spaces.** Undisturbed, dark spaces with abundant paper and fabric are ideal silverfish habitat. Regularly organizing and cleaning basements and closet areas eliminates both shelter and accessible food sources.

**Secure dry goods.** Flour, rice, oatmeal, cereal, and other starch-heavy pantry items should be transferred from their original paper or cardboard packaging into airtight plastic or glass containers.
