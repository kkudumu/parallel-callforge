## Why Shawnee Has a Rodent Problem Worth Taking Seriously

Shawnee sits in the western portion of Johnson County, surrounded by mature green corridors and a growing mix of established neighborhoods and newer residential developments. Shawnee Mission Park—the largest county park in Johnson County at over 1,500 acres—provides year-round habitat for rodents along its wooded edges and creek drainages. When fall temperatures drop, that nearby wildlife pressure doesn't stay in the park.

Clear Creek and other drainage corridors running through the area also act as rodent travel routes, connecting green spaces to residential yards. Bird feeders, compost piles, and unsecured trash along these corridors draw mice and rats to your property perimeter—and from there, into the structure itself.

Older housing stock near downtown Shawnee commonly has aging foundation seals, deteriorated door sweeps, and utility penetrations that were never properly rodent-proofed. These are the entry points rodents exploit season after season, and new owners often inherit the same vulnerabilities the previous family dealt with.

### Common Rodents We Treat

**House Mice** – The most frequent call we receive. House mice squeeze through openings as small as a quarter-inch—gaps around plumbing lines, dryer vents, and foundation cracks are typical access points. They contaminate food storage, chew through electrical wiring, and reproduce fast enough that a small problem becomes a serious infestation within weeks.

**Norway Rats** – Larger and heavier than mice, Norway rats burrow along foundations, under concrete slabs, and in crawl spaces. They're drawn to outdoor food sources near the home—garbage areas, bird feeders, and woodpiles stacked against the exterior.

**Roof Rats** – Less common but present in the metro. Roof rats nest high, in attics and wall voids, and travel along utility lines and overhanging tree branches to access structures in wooded sections of western Johnson County.

## Seasonal Rodent Patterns in Shawnee, KS

Kansas weather creates predictable surges in rodent activity throughout the year. Knowing when pressure peaks gives you a head start on prevention.

### Fall and Winter (October–February)

This is the heaviest period for rodent intrusions in Shawnee. When temperatures drop, mice and rats actively search for warm structures—and residential homes are their primary target. January and February calls spike as homeowners hear scratching inside walls at night or find droppings in kitchen cabinets and pantry areas. Garage entries, dryer vents, and gaps where pipes pass through exterior walls are among the most common access points.

February is also when termite awareness runs high nationally, and for good reason: many homeowners dealing with a winter rodent issue discover other pest pressure during the same inspection.

### Spring (March–May)

As temperatures warm, outdoor rodent activity resumes and breeding accelerates sharply. March is the right time to walk your home's exterior for winter damage—lifted weep holes, foundation cracks, deteriorated caulk around utility lines—and address existing activity before populations multiply. Ant and termite pressure also picks up across Johnson County beginning in March, making spring inspections especially productive.

### Summer and Early Fall (June–September)

Outdoor food sources keep many rodents in the yard during summer months. However, ongoing residential development along Shawnee's western and southern growth corridors regularly displaces rodent populations, pushing them toward established neighborhoods nearby. Calls typically rise again in September and October as temperatures begin to fall and rodents seek warmth ahead of winter.

## Signs You Have a Rodent Problem

Catching an infestation early limits damage and makes treatment more effective. Watch for:

- **Droppings** – Dark, pellet-shaped, found near food storage areas, under sinks, or along baseboards
- **Gnaw marks** – Chewed edges on food packaging, electrical wiring insulation, or wood framing
- **Scratching sounds** – Movement inside walls or ceilings, usually at night
- **Nesting material** – Shredded insulation, paper, or fabric tucked behind appliances or in hidden corners
- **Grease marks** – Dark smear lines along baseboards where rodents travel the same path repeatedly

Multiple signs together mean an active infestation is likely. Rodents rarely travel alone, and populations grow fast without professional intervention.
